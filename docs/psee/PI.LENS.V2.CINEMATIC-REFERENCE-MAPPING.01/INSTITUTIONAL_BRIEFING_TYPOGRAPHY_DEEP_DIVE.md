## 0. WHY THIS REFERENCE

The Financial Times, Bloomberg long-form, The Economist briefings, and the editorial design tradition more broadly are the references for *executive readability under information density*. They demonstrate:

- typography is the primary navigation system for executive readers
- editorial pacing produces comprehension that no UI ladder achieves
- pull-quotes, lead paragraphs, and section transitions are cognitive scaffolding
- a serious reader expects a serious typographic register

LENS borrows the *editorial hierarchy* and *executive reading comfort*. LENS does not borrow the *newspaper density* or the *traditional publishing rigidity*.

---

## 1. CORE QUALITIES TO STUDY

### 1.1 Editorial hierarchy

Headlines, lead paragraphs, body, pull-quotes, section breaks, captions, and footers are deliberately differentiated and serve specific cognitive roles. The reader knows what each tier *is*.

### 1.2 Executive reading comfort

Type is sized, tracked, and leaded for sustained reading. Line lengths are calibrated. Color contrast is optimized for fatigue-free reading.

### 1.3 Narrative pacing

Long-form briefings pace the reader through the material: lead with the headline, follow with the implication, develop with structure, close with confidence statement. The pacing is structural, not stylistic.

### 1.4 Information trustworthiness

The typographic register itself signals trustworthiness. The same facts read differently in a Financial Times layout versus a SaaS card; the layout *is* part of the trust signal.

---

## 2. WHAT LENS BORROWS

- **Tiered editorial hierarchy.** LENS uses a 5–7 tier type system: declaration / implication / propagation lead / body / metadata / caption / micro-label.
- **Reading-comfort calibration.** LENS body type is sized and leaded for executive reading distance, not for analyst screen-density.
- **Narrative pacing.** LENS surfaces lead with the declaration, follow with the implication, develop with the propagation, and close with evidence confidence.
- **Trustworthy register.** LENS typographic register signals institution-grade trust. The reader believes the surface partly because the surface looks believable.

---

## 3. WHAT LENS MUST AVOID

- **Newspaper density.** FT prints columns of body text; LENS does not. The *density* is wrong, even if the *hierarchy* is right.
- **Traditional publishing rigidity.** Publishing has constraints LENS does not have (column widths, fold structure, broadsheet aspect). LENS does not inherit them.
- **Serif as default.** Editorial publishing leans serif; LENS leans humanist sans. The serif tradition is part of the borrow inspiration but not the implementation.
- **Newsroom register.** LENS is not a newspaper article. The register is *executive briefing*, not *editorial reportage*.

---

## 4. TYPOGRAPHIC LADDER FOR LENS (DERIVED)

Mandatory tiers, named:

| Tier             | Role                                       | Approximate scale           |
|------------------|--------------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Declaration      | The single dominant claim                  | 2.5x–3.5x body               |
| Implication      | What it means                              | 1.5x–1.8x body               |
| Propagation lead | Sub-claim about how state moves            | 1.2x–1.4x body               |
| Body             | Operational narrative                      | 1.0x (≈16–18px)              |
| Metadata         | Time-of-truth, source labels               | 0.7x–0.85x                   |
| Micro-label      | Restrained uppercase tier labels           | 0.65x–0.75x, +tracking       |
| Caption          | Evidence references, footnote register     | 0.65x–0.8x                   |

The exact scale is calibrated per surface but the *ladder structure* is fixed.

---

## 5. READING-COMFORT INVARIANTS

LENS body reading must satisfy:

- line-height ≥ 1.45 on body
- line-length 50–75 characters where possible
- contrast ratio ≥ 7:1 on body against ground
- tracking neutral on body, slightly tighter on declaration
- weight 400 default body, 300 acceptable for atmospheric body

A LENS surface where executive body type fails any of these is non-compliant.

---

## 6. UPPERCASE DOCTRINE

Uppercase is permitted only at tier "Micro-label" and only with positive tracking (≥ 0.05em).

Forbidden uppercase usage:

- declarations
- implications
- propagation leads
- body
- metadata
- captions

Editorial publishing uses uppercase for kickers and tier markers; LENS borrows the *kicker* idea (a small, tracked label above the declaration) and rejects all other uppercase usage.

---

## 7. NARRATIVE PACING TEMPLATE

Editorial pacing applied to LENS:

1. **Kicker (micro-label):** the structural domain or context.
2. **Declaration (heading):** what the system is asserting.
3. **Lead implication (sub-heading):** what this means for the operator.
4. **Body narrative:** the operational explanation, in executive language.
5. **Propagation cross-section:** structured detail.
6. **Evidence confidence close:** a final sentence that bounds the claim.

This pacing is the executive briefing form, applied to a 2D surface. LENS surfaces that omit any tier are incomplete.

---

## 8. INSPECTION CUES (FOR LENS REVIEW PASSES)

1. Is the type ladder visible — can the reader name each tier on sight?
2. Is body reading fatigue-free at executive viewport?
3. Has LENS picked up newspaper density? (FAIL — must reduce)
4. Has LENS picked up serif default? (FAIL — must use humanist sans)
5. Is the narrative pacing complete — kicker → declaration → implication → body → cross-section → close?

---

## 9. THE EDITORIAL LESSON LENS MUST INTERNALIZE

The deepest lesson from institutional briefings:

> **Hierarchy is the navigation system. Components are secondary.**

A LENS surface where the reader navigates by component identity (cards, panels, charts) has failed the editorial contract. A LENS surface where the reader navigates by typographic tier (kicker → declaration → implication) has succeeded.

---

## 10. SUMMARY

The editorial / FT gift to LENS is one phrase: **"Hierarchy carries trust; pacing carries comprehension."**

That phrase is borrowed entire.

Newspaper density, serif default, and traditional publishing structure are not borrowed.
